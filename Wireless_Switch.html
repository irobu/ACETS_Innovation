Wireless Switch

Receiver and transmitter. Both units contain rechargeable Li-Ion batteries, indicate charging and battery status as well as the activity status of each channel

The transmitter has two jack inputs and allows for use of two switches simultaneously. The receiver can be placed up to 10m away from transmitter

Wireless switch in use. Two switches are plugged into transmitter and the two output cables from the receiver are plugged into the switch input of a communication device

Project: Design and manufacture of a pair of 2-channel rechargeable transmitter-receiver devices with power saving capabilities that can be used when wireless switches are required for switch placement. 

Scenario/Client Description: Five year old boy using a communication device with two-switch step scanning access in various physical positions (i.e., wheelchair, standing frame, special tomato seat, floor). Client is diagnosed with Pelizaeus-Merzbacher Disease, is non-ambulatory, has gross uncontrolled arm movements, and is able to use thumbs when arms are stabilized on tray. Wireless switches were required to prevent switch cables from being pulled from switch base, and to accommodate for varying positions of switch use. 

Challanges:  The speech generating communication device (SGCD) identified as most appropriate for this child’s developmental language level did not offer connectivity to commercially available wireless switch interfaces. Commercially available wireless switches did not match this child’s physical or sensory abilities.  

Plan of Action:  The system is composed of two units built around programmable microcontrollers and wireless transmission modules. Each module incorporates a lithium-ion battery and a charging controller. A power management protocol is programmed into the modules for maximum energy saving. Two independent channels allow for simultaneous use of two switches connected to the transmitter. A successful transmission range of at least 10m was acquired. 

Client Outcomes/Feedback:  This child had a successful trial with the identified SGCD and custom wireless switch interface. Wireless connectivity prevented this child’s fingers from being caught in the wires, protected the switches from damage, and allowed the parents flexibility in positioning the switches across contexts. 

Reflections/Future Work:  When using this same SGCD and access method in future interactions, this wireless switch interface will be considered again. Additionally, in instances where a specific position or style of switch is required, and switch cables interfere with client’s use of the switch, this wireless interface will be considered to allow for maximum flexibility. 

Date:  June 2016
